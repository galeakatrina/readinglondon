revealing the untamed

a celebration of london's hidden ecologies

katrina galea

w1803133

This project aims to look at ‘leftover’ spaces within the city, seeing them as territories of potential for natural processes rather than as useless remnants. They are fragments which are often overlooked, yet are embedded in the fabric of our everyday lives. Compared to the well-maintained and manicured public spaces within a fast-growing city, these pockets are a celebration of the untamed - they serve as subtle reminders of the pure natural processes of growth, migration and decay. Just as the buildings erected, the people present and the events that happen within, the presence of untamed nature occupying leftover spaces also contributes to a place’s memory.

Gilles Clément identifies these spaces as the Third Landscape, the sum of all the human-disturbed land through which natural processes still occur. (Clément, G., 1999). The Third Landscape designates the sum of the space left over by man to landscape evolution – to nature alone. This constitues transitional spaces, abandoned land and ruins, roadsides, railroad embankments, shores, and more.

For this project, a few spots around the second urban walk presented in the module (the walk around Canning Town and Silvertown) were identified - zones in which different kinds of leftover spaces are examples of Third Landscapes. These sites vary in kind, scale and constituents, yet are all equally important examples of nature reclaiming those spaces which may be neglected and forgotten by man.

The toolkit presented in this virtual sketchbook aims to reveal these ecosystems and highlight their presence - by attempting to recreate an ‘x-ray vision’, cutting through the layers of the built to reveal new hybrids of the natural and the man-made.

process & precedents

The idea behind this project stemmed from a process that I personally have already been using to document my time here in London. Collecting fragments of botanical specimen has been a method of keeping track of the places I have visited so far, while also serving as indicators of colour palettes and sensory experiences these places offered at the time. It is a process which is highly subjective, as the choice of specimen is very particular and personal. It is a process which creates, however, a very intimate connection between self and site.

At the sites identified, a series of specimen were collected in order to create mini-botanical arrangements representing the area. The choice of species was highly personal; the choice was based on creating a varied palette of textures, colours and forms, but also made upon observation of species were the most dominant or which particularly stood out in the space to reflect its character. Personal preferences and tastes should also be taken into account as the invisible protagonists which heavily predetermine a degree of choice. The specimen were then documented and identified in order to create a library of species that each of these sites hosted, during the time I experienced the space myself. This resulted in a series of tactile psychogeographic ‘collections’ of these ecologies.

In order to reveal these ecologies in their belonging sites, I adopted the technique of cyanotype printing in order to simulate the process of revealing the unseen and forgotten. Through the combination of producing negatives of photographs and overlaying the actual specimen collected, a collection of prints was created, showcasing these ecologies and the way they relate to the built environment.

precedents

Gilles Clément, The Planetary Garden, 1999

"The Planetary Garden" is an environmental manifesto that outlines Clément's interpretation of the laws that govern the natural world and the principles that should guide our stewardship of the global garden of Earth. These are among the tenets of a humanist ecology, which posits that the natural world and humankind cannot be understood as separate from one another. The texts make a powerful statement about the nature of the world and humanity's place within it.

Michelle Rozic, Tidelands, 2013

“In Tidelands, rocks are wrapped with cyanotype prints of litter and ocean flora washed up near the Santa Monica Pier, a Los Angeles landmark. Rocks gathered from the Llano Del Rio Colony site, a failed alternative community whose ruins are located in the high desert north of Los Angeles, are printed on with imagery from plastic bags and wrappers. The ocean and desert are united in this work, two distant yet similar physical locations without potable water” (Rozic,2014).

Nick Papadimitriou, Scarp, 2012

Part meditation on nature and walking, part memoir and part social history, Nick Papadimitriou’s book is a personal inquiry into the spirit of a place: a 14-mile broken ridge of land on the fringes of Northern London known as Scarp. Conspicuous but largely forgotten, a vast yet largely invisible presence hovering just beyond the metropolis, Scarp is a vast storehouse of regional memory. Papadimitriou explores and reimagines this brooding, pregnant landscape, meticulously observing his surroundings, finding surprising connections and revealing lost slices of the past.
